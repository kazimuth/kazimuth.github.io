Visualizing Functions and Relations, Part 1

Introduction

Many people seem to think that math is boring, unpleasant, and difficult to understand. These people are right; but I wish they weren’t.

It’s important to understand that math is basically just a pen-and-paper board game, like dungeons and dragons. The rules of math are carefully designed to simulate the real world. It’s not magic.

But math isn’t taught this way. It’s taught as an strict and arcane collection of rules that have to be memorized, on pain of bad test scores. Not much time is spent helping students understand why we choose these rules. A number of people are trying to change this ([1] [2] [3] [4] [5]). There’s a growing wave of math resources focusing on intuitive, sensory engagement with math.

I think resources like this are especially needed for modern pure math. For about a century, the style in professional math has been:

  • no pictures in publications
  • all notation as concise and abstract as possible
  • use as many fancy tricks to describe your problem as you can think of
  • leave as much unsaid as possible

This makes pure math seem much scarier than it really is. I want to make it friendlier.

This series will be an introduction to some important playing pieces in pure math: functions and relations. My overall goal is to convince you that functions look like this:

We’ll get an intuitive understanding of what this means, and learn how to prove it – how to use it as a strategy in the game of formal math, if a pro mathematician challenges you to a duel.

Let’s begin.

What’s a relation?

First, we need to define our playing pieces. The most basic piece we’ll use is called a binary relation. Before we start writing out equations and strict mathematical rules, let’s try to get a general sense of what a binary relation looks like. There are multiple ways to draw them, but they’re all connected.

Diagram type one: Directed Graphs

My favorite way to draw binary relations is as directed graphs, or digraphs, which look like this:

A digraph is a collection of nodes – labeled circles – and a collection of edges – little arrows going between the nodes. We can model all sorts of situations with digraphs. For instance, a family tree can be represented as a digraph. We draw a node for each name and then draw arrows from parents to children:

We can also draw digraphs of love triangles, by drawing edges from people to people they like:

Notice that nodes are allowed to have edges to themselves. Also, nodes don’t have to have any edges. In the “Romantic Comedy” example, C is a sidekick who’s not interested in A or B.

We can draw a digraph of which classes use which books in a school:

Notice how this digraph is divided into two parts – the classes and the books. All the edges go from classes to books. Graphs like this are called “bipartite”, which means “two parts”.

Equivalent diagrams

We now have three types of diagrams: digraphs, two column tables, and plots.

I want to convince you that these types of diagrams are equivalent: you can convert between them as needed, without losing information.

To convince each other of things, mathematicians write proofs. A “proof” is a sequence of moves in a board game. The basic moves in the board game are supposed to be so simple that everybody agrees they should be allowed. Bigger, more complicated moves are built up out of simple moves. (In this case, the moves would be things like converting between diagrams.)

Sadly, I can’t prove anything yet. I haven’t explained any of the basic moves! We’ll get to that over the next couple posts. I still want to convince you though. Mathematicians have a classic trick for this:

Equality & Stretchiness

There’s one more thing I want to cover in this post. Before, I said that two digraphs are considered to be “the same” if they have the same nodes and the same connections, even if the nodes are arranged differently.

A more intuitive way to explain this is to say that:

Digraphs are stretchy.

So for instance, we would consider these three digraphs to be the same, because we can rearrange and stretch them into each other:

To write down that two things are the same, we put an equals sign between them:

(We could also use a three-directional equal sign:

But we haven’t talked about ternary relations yet.)

It might seem a little strange to put an equals sign between things that aren’t exactly the same. But we actually do this all the time in math. Consider the equation:

1 + 1

=

2

The things on either side of the equal sign here aren’t exactly the same – they’re just the same enough for our purposes. You could also think of them as different ways to accomplish the same thing. To get a “2”, you can take two “1"s and add them together, or you can just write down a “2”.

In the case of our digraphs, we consider the above diagrams to be different ways to draw the same digraph. Since the digraph is stretchy, it doesn’t matter how it’s laid out in space.

We can come up with similar rules for our other diagram types. For example, I declare that our tables are unordered: we agree not to care about the order of the rows. So we can write:

Functions are ubiquitous in pure math. They have a simple definition, but are used in all sorts of ways. Functions can be used for:

  • computing
  • labeling
  • grouping
  • forgetting
  • embedding
  • rearranging
  • …and more.

We’ll learn how to unpack the definition of functions to see the rich structure it implies. We’ll do this intuitively – with lots of pictures – to feel comfortable and confident in our understanding. But we’ll also do it formally: we’ll learn how to express our understanding as moves in a strict board game, to double-check our intuition and share it with others.

Note for mathematicians (don't read this if you're not a mathematician!!)

We’ll be proving the “trivial homomorphism theorem”: “Input-total equality-preserving binary relations are bijections between a partition of their domain and a subset of their codomain.” We’ll work with a mixture of classical and diagrammatic first-order logic. The diagrammatic logic will be an n-ary generalization of binary relation algebras [6] viewed as strict 2-categories, using spiders [7] [8] to represent relational joins.
